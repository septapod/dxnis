## Research Synthesis (2026-04-10, after three deep-research tracks)

### Track 1: B2B consulting website best practices
- **"Clarity before cleverness" traces to Steve Krug, Ogilvy, and Claude Hopkins.** Cleverness earned on top of clarity is fine. Cleverness that replaces clarity is malpractice. My prior drafts were the second thing.
- **The website's job is not to close.** It's to book qualified calls AND repel bad-fit prospects. Both functions matter.
- **April Dunford's rule:** Position against the buyer's actual competitive alternatives. For Brent, those are: "do nothing," "hire a big strategy firm," "use our internal team," "send execs to a conference," "wait another quarter."
- **StoryBrand grunt test:** A caveman should understand, in 5 seconds, who you help, what they get, and what to do next.
- **CTA research:** "Book a 20-minute fit call" is the strongest candidate for high-ticket B2B. But on solo-advisor sites specifically, the primary CTA is almost always a newsletter signup, not a call.
- **Trust signals ranked:** Named client logos > named executive testimonials with photos > specific outcome statements > publications/speaking > volume proxies > credentials.

### Track 2: Credit union executive buyer psychology
- **The biggest finding: there is a gap in the market.** Not one boutique CU consultant leads with AI above the fold. Hofheimer leads with "ambitious credit unions who want to change the world." Cornerstone leads with scale. Humanidei leads with people ops. Filene is a research institute. "Credit union AI strategy" is an empty seat and Brent can take it.
- **Brent's moat: receipts.** Other CU consultants talk about AI. Brent actually deploys it weekly. Newsletter, workshops with working tools, uses the tools in his own practice. That's the "I've seen this movie" signal C-suite buyers need.
- **CU leaders already have names for the pain:**
  - **"Groundhog Day"** (Mark Arnold) — annual planning cycles that repeat without shipping
  - **"10% Comments"** (CUInsight) — the last 10% of what people are thinking that doesn't get said in the room, where real growth actually lives
  - **Focus as the #1 barrier** — Filene survey: 20% of CU leaders say focus beats money, talent, and technology as the top barrier
  - **Provider trust** — Filene: 80% of CU leaders say a strong provider network is a must-have, 70% say it's hard to assess trustworthiness
- **Shevlin has already softened the beach.** He's publicly telling CU CEOs their AI maturity is inflated: "There is no AI strategy without a data strategy" and "many institutions remain far less mature than they think." He's the accuser. Brent can be the answer. "Once you believe Ron, here's what you do about it."
- **CU executives screen for:** industry specificity (track record at their asset tier), neutrality, cooperative-model fluency. Say "members" not "customers." Reference "$200M to $3B" or whatever range Brent actually serves.
- **Buying committee reality:** CEO holds the pen. Board chair can veto. CFO objects on cost. The homepage needs to give the CEO language to defend the hire to the board.
- **Productized shape:** Hofheimer runs a fractional-CSO retainer. That's a shape CU CEOs already recognize. Brent's retainer could productize similarly as a fractional AI strategy officer.

### Track 3: Boutique consultant website teardowns
- **Closest template: Jonathan Stark.** Solo consultant, expensive advice to experts, warm first-person voice, newsletter as primary CTA, named-outcome subhead. His site is the direct structural match for what Brent should build.
- **Second template: April Dunford.** Interspersed testimonials (not a ghettoized carousel), named-executive social proof, flat non-clever H1, first-person voice.
- **Anti-positioning filter.** Basecamp ("not big, sluggish ones"). Hofheimer ("highly ambitious credit unions"). Philip Morgan ("sell through trust, reputation, and professional judgment"). Each actively repels the wrong buyer. Brent should have one.
- **No pricing on homepage. Ever.** Zero of the twelve reviewed advisory sites showed prices. Brent's instinct to keep pricing off is validated.
- **Primary CTA is a newsletter, not a call.** Stark, Godin, McKenzie, Vinh all drive to email. Dunford drives to contact. None drive to a "Book a Discovery Call" button. Brent should make AI for FIs the primary CTA, with a secondary contact option for warm inbound.
- **Books are the #1 trust signal on strong solo-advisor sites.** Brent doesn't have a book. His newsletter is the closest equivalent. Treat it that way: prominent, named, with a subscriber count.
- **Named client testimonials beat logo grids for peer industries.** The CU world is small. A named quote from a CU CEO at $500M+ is worth more than a generic logo wall. Brent has four named clients and two strong testimonials already (Ray Springsteen at Abound, Mary Beth Spuck at Resource One).
- **Quantified social proof.** Patrick McKenzie: "$15M+ in raises negotiated due to this essay." Jonathan Stark: "10k+ daily readers." Find a number that proves the work matters and put it in the hero.

### What this means for dxn.is specifically
1. **Name "credit union" in the first five words of the H1.** Both magnet (CU visitors recognize themselves) and filter (non-CU visitors bounce, which is good).
2. **Lead with the AI gap no competitor owns.** Position as "AI strategy for credit unions" first, strategy facilitation second. The gap is real and the market already has vocabulary for the pain.
3. **Use Jonathan Stark's first-person voice.** "Hi, I'm Brent Dixon." One human, warm, direct.
4. **Primary CTA becomes AI for FIs newsletter, not "Let's Talk."** Secondary CTA becomes a contact form or calendar link.
5. **Name the four CU clients** (Abound, TRUE, Brewery, Chartway) in the About section explicitly. Named clients are the #1 trust signal for peer CU CEOs.
6. **Add a single hero-adjacent testimonial** (Ray Springsteen's "most strategic strategic planning sessions" line is perfect) right under the hero, before scroll.
7. **Keep the line "I run the process so your team can focus on the decisions."** It passed every research-backed test. It's the strongest line already on the site.
8. **Add one anti-positioning filter** somewhere above the fold. Something like "for credit unions past the talking stage" or "I work with credit unions that want their AI plan to ship, not just look good on a slide."

**Goal:** Upgrade the website to support the Monday sales push (George Moment outreach) without locking in pricing, without abandoning the design work that's already been done, and without breaking what's working.

### Positioning correction (2026-04-10, Brent's fourth round)

The prior three options over-pivoted to AI. Brent pushed back: he is not ready to narrow his practice to "AI strategy" as the headline offering. AI is the differentiator, not the category. The reasons:

1. **He doesn't want to alienate CUs that want strategic planning facilitation or general strategy work**, which is still the bulk of his practice and pipeline.
2. **His actual worldview is that agentic AI is an org problem, not a tech problem.** He bangs this drum consistently. Calling his practice "AI strategy" contradicts his own belief that AI work can't be separated from strategy work.
3. **He doesn't yet know what a productized "AI strategy" project would look like.** That's a real gap. Offering something he can't yet deliver would be dishonest.

**The correct positioning**: narrow on industry (credit unions), broad on problem (strategy work), with AI literacy as a differentiator built into the voice and POV rather than into the category label.

```
H1:       Strategy facilitation for credit union leadership teams.

Subhead:  I'm Brent Dixon. I help CU boards and executive teams work
          through annual planning and the AI questions that are making
          everything harder right now. My clients leave aligned on a
          plan they own.

Third:    I run the process so your team can focus on the decisions.

Primary CTA:    Read AI for FIs   (→ newsletter signup)
Secondary CTA:  Work with Brent   (→ /contact)
```

**Why this works:**
- H1 names the core practice (strategy facilitation) and the audience (CU leadership teams). Closest to what the live site says today, which protects existing SEO and brand recognition.
- Subhead adds Brent's name (warm, first-person), the two main problem areas (annual planning, AI questions), and the outcome (aligned on a plan they own).
- "The AI questions that are making everything harder right now" captures the moment without positioning the whole practice as AI-only.
- Two items in the subhead, not three. No triplet.
- "A plan they own" is concrete ownership language. Not a worn phrase.

### Proposed Option B (warmer, POV-forward)

```
H1:       Hi, I'm Brent Dixon.

Subhead:  I help credit union leadership teams do strategy work,
          including the AI questions most strategy consultants
```

**Why this works:**

- "A thinking partner who uses the tools every day" is the receipts signal. Concrete and verifiable.
- Names the competitive alternative ("most strategy consultants") which is Dunford's positioning move.
- One small risk: the line about other strategy consultants could read as throwing shade. If that bothers you, the softer version is "most strategy consultants don't use these tools in their own practice yet."

### Proposed Option C (POV-forward, names the drum you already bang)

```
H1:       Your CU's hardest decisions are strategy problems.
          Even the AI ones.

Subhead:  I'm Brent Dixon. I help credit union boards and executive
          teams work through annual planning and the cross-cutting
          questions AI keeps surfacing. Clients leave with a plan

Third:    I run the process so your team can focus on the decisions.

Primary CTA:    Read AI for FIs   (→ newsletter signup)
```

**Why this works:**
- H1 names your actual worldview in two sentences. First sentence is the claim. Second sentence is the short punch that makes it specific and current. This is build-and-drop rhythm from the brent-voice skill.
- "Even the AI ones" lands the POV that agentic AI is org strategy, not tech strategy. That's the drum you bang in public. Putting it in the H1 means every visitor meets your POV before they meet your services.
- Subhead anchors the POV in concrete work ("annual planning and the cross-cutting questions AI keeps surfacing") and names the outcome.
- Strongest on point of view. No competitor can copy this without being dishonest about their own practice.

### Which option I'd pick and why

**Option C** is my pick now. It names your actual POV in the H1, which is the single thing no competitor can replicate. The whole research synthesis kept surfacing "differentiate on a defensible angle." Your defensible angle is the belief that AI work is strategy work. Say it out loud above the fold.

**Option A** is the safest if you're not ready to lead with POV and want the site to feel close to what's there today.

**Option B** is the warmest and most Jonathan Stark-template.

None of these three narrow you to AI-only. All three keep strategy facilitation as the core and treat AI as a distinguishing expertise.

## Structural changes the research surfaced (beyond copy)

These aren't text rewrites. They're layout and IA changes the research suggests, ranked by impact. I'm flagging them so you can decide what's in scope for Monday versus a later pass.

### 1. Swap the primary CTA from "Let's Talk" to "Read AI for FIs" (high impact)
Every strong solo-advisor site in the teardown used newsletter signup as the primary CTA. The research on trust-building for high-ticket advisory is unambiguous: buyers need 3–10 touches before a discovery call, and the newsletter is the lightest-friction first touch. "Let's Talk" asks for commitment the visitor isn't ready to give. Newsletter signup lets the visitor self-select into the trust loop at their own pace. The "Work with Brent" contact link stays as a secondary CTA for warm inbound.

### 2. Add a hero-adjacent testimonial (high impact)
April Dunford's site puts a single named-executive testimonial at the fold edge. The research is clear that C-suite buyers need a named-peer signal immediately, not buried in a carousel. Ray Springsteen's "most strategic strategic planning sessions we've ever had" is the strongest line in the testimonial set and would land hardest here.

**Proposed placement:** right below the hero, above the client logo marquee, as a single testimonial block with photo, name, title, CU. One sentence max.

### 3. Name the clients explicitly in the About section (medium impact)
Current About section mentions "credit unions and community financial institutions." That's abstract. The research on CU peer buyers says named clients are the #1 trust signal. Replace with: "I've worked with leadership teams at Abound Credit Union, TRUE Community Credit Union, Brewery Credit Union, and Chartway Federal Credit Union." Concrete, specific, peer-verifiable.

### 4. Subscriber count: hold until 1000+
Decision: Brent is holding on publishing the AI for FIs subscriber count until the list crosses three figures (1,000+). Until then, the site uses qualitative phrasing like "read weekly by credit union leaders" rather than a specific number. Revisit this when the list grows.

### 5. Consider an anti-positioning filter line (medium impact)
Basecamp: "not big, sluggish ones." Hofheimer: "highly ambitious credit unions." Philip Morgan: "businesses that sell through trust, reputation, and professional judgment." Each actively repels the wrong buyer.

**Candidate filter lines for you to react to:**
- "I work with credit unions that want their AI plan to ship, not just look good on a slide."
- "For credit union CEOs past the talking stage on AI."
- "For CU leadership teams that want a plan they'll execute, not another binder."

This could live in the subhead (already handled in Hero Option B) or as a standalone line in the About section.

### 6. De-ghettoize the testimonial carousel (lower impact, bigger effort)
Dunford's site intersperses named testimonials throughout the scroll instead of clustering them. The research says this creates recurring validation as the visitor reads. This is a structural redesign, not a copy change. Flagging as a future pass, not a Monday item.

### 7. Consider a "who this is for" yes/no block on service pages (lower impact)
When you build out individual service pages (if you ever do), the research pattern is:
- H1 naming the outcome
- "Who this is for" yes/no list (explicit filters)
- "What you walk away with" (deliverables, not activities)
- One testimonial specific to that service
- CTA (email or newsletter)

### 2. The "what does an AI project actually look like?" gap (strategic, not urgent)

Brent said honestly: "I still don't know if a credit union came to me and said, 'Help me build out my AI strategy,' at this moment I don't actually know what that project would look like."

This is a productization gap, and it's the single most important strategic work Brent needs to do to make the website copy land. You can't write clear copy for an offering you can't yet describe in plain language to yourself.

**This isn't a Monday item.** It's a strategic conversation worth booking time for separately. Candidate framing questions for that conversation:

- What does the buyer's week look like before the engagement? What does it look like after?
- Who's in the room when the work happens?
- What artifacts does the CU have at the end?
- What's the minimum viable version (a single workshop? a two-week diagnostic? a 90-day plan?) and what's the full version (a 12-month fractional arrangement?)
- What's the handoff to the CU's internal team look like?
- What does Brent NOT do? (Implementation? Vendor selection? Data cleanup? Explicit exclusions help.)
- What happens if the CU decides mid-engagement that they need something different?
- What does success look like at 30, 60, 90 days?
- Who else has done something like this successfully that Brent could study?
