The basics

What is an eval?

An eval is a test that measures whether your AI did a good job.

An eval is just an evaluation. If you have ever run a performance review, you already know how it works. You decide what good looks like, then you check the work against it. The only new part is that the work was done by software, not a person.

You give the AI a task. You check the answer against what a good answer looks like. You score it. You do this over and over, on purpose, instead of reading a few replies and hoping. (An LLM is a large language model, the kind of AI behind chat assistants.)

Good AI products are the ones that keep getting better. Evals measure whether the AI is good, so every change is a decision instead of a guess.

The heart of the method

Three levels of evaluation.

Hamel sorts evals into three levels. They get more expensive and slower as you climb, so you run the cheap ones constantly and the costly ones rarely. Build the bottom level first. Open each one to read it.

In plain terms: a simple rule that the AI's answer either passes or fails, checked by a computer in a fraction of a second. You write a lot of them and run them every time you change anything. You know they are strong when the AI struggles to pass them.

For financial institutions, the reply must never show a full account number or a Social Security number, even though that data sits in the system behind the scenes. One automatic check scans every answer for those patterns and fails it if they appear.

How often: every change Who: automated Cost: very low

In plain terms: some quality is a judgment call. Was the answer clear? Was the tone right? Did it help? A person reads real conversations and labels them good or bad. Then you train a second AI to grade at scale, and you keep it honest by checking it against the people.

Start simple. Label each conversation just good or bad. Skip one-to-ten scores at first. A spreadsheet is enough to line up your AI grader with a human reviewer. If almost all your conversations are fine, "the AI agrees with the human ninety percent of the time" can fool you. Track how well it catches the bad ones, not just overall agreement.

How often: a regular cadence Who: people, then AI Cost: moderate

In plain terms: show two versions to real members and measure which one produces better results. This is called A/B testing. You split people into two groups, give each a different version, and compare what happens.

This is the most expensive level because it uses real members and real time. Save it for when the product is mature and you are confident enough to put it in front of people.

How often: after big changes Who: real members Cost: highest
If you remember one thing

Look at your data.

The AI products that get good are the ones whose teams read their own conversations, a lot of them, all the time.

"You are doing it wrong if you aren't looking at lots of data."

Hamel Husain

When you start, read everything. Over time you can read a sample. Keep reading until you stop learning anything new. No software reads the data for you.

Vendor scorecard

Score a vendor or partner before you commit.

Hamel's method teaches teams how to build evals. These eight questions put it to work when you are buying, not building. A vendor that runs evals can answer all eight without flinching. Each one shows what a good answer and a dodge sound like.

Strong means clear and specific, with something you can see. Vague means they talked around it. Dodged means they changed the subject.

Vendor: ______________________________Date: __________________
1A demo shows the AI at its best. How do you check it holds up on the messy questions real members ask?

A good answer: they test it against lots of real member questions, including the hard ones, and show how often it gets them right. A dodge: they re-run the demo or talk up the technology.

2What kinds of mistakes do you automatically check for before anything reaches a member?

A good answer: a specific list, like wrong information, a rude tone, or leaked data, checked every time they change the AI. A dodge: they call it accurate without naming what they check.

StrongVagueDodged
3How often does someone on your team read real member conversations?

A good answer: on a set schedule, and they can name a number. A dodge: they say the system flags problems on its own, or that they look as needed.

StrongVagueDodged
4If you use AI to check the AI's work, how do you make sure that checker is right?

A good answer: they compare the AI checker against real people and fix it when the two disagree. A dodge: they treat the AI checking its own work as proof.

StrongVagueDodged
5Can you show me your error rate going down over time as you fixed things?

A good answer: real numbers or a chart with dates, heading down. A dodge: a single accuracy number, or a vague claim that it keeps improving.

StrongVagueDodged
6When a member gets a bad answer, how do you find out what went wrong?

A good answer: they pull up that exact conversation and trace the cause quickly. A dodge: they say they would look into it, but cannot say how.

StrongVagueDodged
7How do you keep account numbers and Social Security numbers from showing up in a reply?

A good answer: an automatic check that catches that data before a member sees it. A dodge: they say the AI is trained not to, with no backstop.

StrongVagueDodged
8Will our data be used to train models that other companies use?

A good answer: a clear no, in writing. A dodge: vague reassurance, or that it is buried in the contract.
